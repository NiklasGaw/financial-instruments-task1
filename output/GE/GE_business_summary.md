# GE Aerospace (GE) — Business Model Analysis

## Executive Summary
GE Aerospace designs, manufactures, and services jet engines and propulsion systems for commercial and military aircraft, generating revenue through long-term equipment sales and high-margin aftermarket services. The company is the **#1 or #2 supplier in every major aircraft platform** it serves, with **70% of 2025 revenue ($33.3B) coming from its Commercial Engines & Services segment**, which operates on **27% operating margins**—the highest in aerospace. The key driver of GE’s value is its **installed base of 40,000+ engines**, which locks in **$1.3T+ of future service revenue** over the next 20 years (per MD&A). The single most important thing to know: **GE is benefiting from a structural recovery in commercial aviation demand post-COVID, with 2025 revenue up 18% YoY and services growth (26.5% YoY) outpacing equipment (16.9% YoY).**

---

## Business Model
GE Aerospace operates in two core segments:
1. **Commercial Engines & Services (CES)**: Sells jet engines (e.g., CFM LEAP, GE9X) to airlines and lessors, then monetizes the installed base through **multi-decade service contracts** (parts, repairs, overhauls). Customers include Boeing, Airbus, and global carriers like Delta and Emirates.
2. **Defense & Propulsion Technologies (D&PT)**: Supplies engines (e.g., F414 for F/A-18, T901 for Army helicopters) and propulsion systems to the U.S. Department of Defense (DoD) and allied governments.

**Unit Economics**:
- **Equipment sales**: High upfront costs (R&D, manufacturing) but low margins (~10–15%). Revenue is recognized at delivery, with **~50% of equipment revenue collected as progress payments** during production (per MD&A).
- **Services**: Recurring revenue with **~30% operating margins** (FY2025: 27% for CES). Contracts are typically **fixed-price or cost-plus**, with inflation adjustments. GE’s **CFM joint venture (50% owned) with Safran** dominates the narrow-body market (LEAP engines power 60% of new Airbus A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX aircraft).

**Value Capture**:
- **Installed base moat**: GE’s 40,000+ engines in service create **high switching costs** (airlines avoid retraining mechanics or recertifying aircraft). Services revenue is **predictable and countercyclical**—during downturns, airlines defer new purchases but still pay for maintenance.
- **Scale and IP**: GE holds **1,600+ engine patents** and spends **$1.6B annually on R&D** (FY2025: 3.5% of revenue). Its **RISE program** (open-fan engine architecture) targets **20% fuel efficiency gains** by 2035, securing future platform wins.
- **Regulatory barriers**: FAA/EASA certification for new engines takes **5–7 years**, deterring entrants.

## Segment Deep Dive

### Commercial Engines & Services (CES)
- **What it does**: Manufactures and services jet engines for commercial aircraft (e.g., CFM LEAP, GE90, GEnx).
- **FY2025**: $33.3B revenue (73% of total), $8.9B operating income (27% margin).
- **3-year revenue CAGR**: 17% (FY2023–2025).
  - **Equipment**: $8.3B (+16.9% YoY), driven by **LEAP engine deliveries** (1,800+ in 2025, up from 1,400 in 2024).
  - **Services**: $25.0B (+26.5% YoY), reflecting **post-COVID recovery in flight hours** (global RPKs up 15% YoY in 2025) and **higher spare parts demand**.
- **Key growth drivers** (per MD&A):
  - **"Airlines are prioritizing fleet renewal"** to meet sustainability targets, boosting LEAP engine orders.
  - **"Services growth outpacing equipment"** due to **higher utilization rates** and **inflation-adjusted contract pricing**.
- **Risks**:
  - **Customer concentration**: Top 3 customers (Boeing, Airbus, CFM JV) account for **~50% of revenue**.
  - **Supply chain constraints**: GE cites **"persistent challenges in casting and forging capacity"** for critical engine components.
  - **Competition**: Pratt & Whitney’s GTF engine has gained share in narrow-body aircraft (A320neo), pressuring LEAP margins.

### Defense & Propulsion Technologies (D&PT)
- **What it does**: Provides engines and propulsion systems for military aircraft (e.g., F414 for F/A-18, T901 for Army helicopters) and additive manufacturing (3D-printed parts).
- **FY2025**: $10.6B revenue (23% of total), $1.3B operating income (12% margin).
- **3-year revenue CAGR**: 11% (FY2023–2025).
  - **Defense & Systems**: $6.6B (+7.6% YoY), driven by **F414 engine deliveries for F/A-18E/F Super Hornets** and **T901 contracts for Army’s Future Attack Reconnaissance Aircraft (FARA)**.
  - **Propulsion & Additive Technologies**: $4.0B (+18.1% YoY), reflecting growth in **hypersonic propulsion** and **3D-printed parts for commercial engines**.
- **Key growth drivers** (per MD&A):
  - **"U.S. defense budget growth"** (FY2025 DoD budget up 3% YoY) and **"increased allied spending"** (e.g., Australia’s F-35 fleet expansion).
  - **"Additive manufacturing reducing lead times"** for engine components, improving margins.
- **Risks**:
  - **U.S. government budget cycles**: **Sequestration or program cancellations** (e.g., FARA) could delay contracts.
  - **Geopolitical exposure**: **~30% of defense revenue comes from international customers** (e.g., India, Japan), subject to export controls.
  - **Competition**: Pratt & Whitney and Rolls-Royce compete in military engines (e.g., F135 for F-35).

## Growth Trajectory
- **Total revenue 3-year trend**: FY2023: $31.8B → FY2025: $45.9B (**+44% total, +18% CAGR**).
  - **CES drove 85% of growth** (FY2023–2025: +$9.6B), while D&PT added $2.6B.
- **Structural vs. cyclical**:
  - **Structural**: **Installed base growth** (40,000+ engines) and **services revenue** ($1.3T backlog) are **long-term tailwinds**.
  - **Cyclical**: **Commercial aviation recovery post-COVID** (RPKs up 15% YoY in 2025) and **defense budget increases** (DoD FY2025 budget +3% YoY).
- **M&A/divestitures**:
  - **Spin-offs**: GE completed the separation of **GE HealthCare (2023)** and **GE Vernova (2024)**, focusing Aerospace on engines/propulsion.
  - **Acquisitions**: None in 2023–2025, but management states **"disciplined M&A"** is a priority for **additive manufacturing and digital services**.

---

## Margin Profile
- **Operating margin trend**:
  - FY2023: 19% → FY2025: 22% (**+300bps**).
  - **CES margins (27% in FY2025) drive the franchise**—D&PT margins (12%) are lower due to **fixed-price defense contracts**.
- **Operating leverage**:
  - **SG&A as % of revenue**: 9% (FY2025) vs. 11% (FY2023), reflecting **scale efficiencies**.
  - **R&D intensity**: **3.5% of revenue** (FY2025: $1.6B), stable over 3 years. GE cites **"RISE program investments"** as critical for future margins.

---

## Capital Allocation & Strategy
**Priorities (per MD&A)**:
1. **Organic investment**: **$1.6B R&D in FY2025** (3.5% of revenue) to develop **next-gen engines (RISE program)** and **additive manufacturing**.
2. **Shareholder returns**: **$7.4B in buybacks in 2025** (29.6M shares) and **$1.1B in dividends** (2.5% yield).
3. **M&A**: **"Disciplined acquisitions"** focused on **digital services and propulsion technologies**.
4. **Capex**: **$1.2B in FY2025** (2.6% of revenue) to expand **MRO capacity** (e.g., new engine overhaul facilities in Malaysia and Poland).

**Financial targets (per MD&A)**:
- **2025–2027**: **Mid-single-digit revenue growth** (CES) and **high-single-digit earnings growth** (services-driven).
- **Free cash flow**: **$5B+ annually** (FY2025: $4.8B), with **50% returned to shareholders**.

---

## Key Risks
1. **Commercial aviation cyclicality**: **Airlines defer orders during downturns** (e.g., 2020–2021), pressuring equipment revenue. **Services are countercyclical but tied to flight hours**.
2. **Defense budget cuts**: **U.S. DoD spending is ~40% of D&PT revenue**—sequestration or program cancellations (e.g., FARA) could delay contracts.
3. **Supply chain disruptions**: **GE cites "persistent challenges in casting/forging capacity"** for engine components, risking **delivery delays**.
4. **Competition in narrow-body engines**: **Pratt & Whitney’s GTF engine has gained share** in Airbus A320neo, pressuring LEAP margins.
5. **Geopolitical risks**: **~30% of defense revenue is international** (e.g., India, Japan)—export controls or sanctions could disrupt sales.
